Prime rib

Prime rib is a popular and indulgent cut of beef that is tender, juicy, and full of flavor.

Prime rib is a delicious and decadent cut of beef that is prized for its rich flavor, tenderness, and juiciness. This popular cut of meat comes from the rib section of the cow, where the meat is marbled with fat and has a delicious, buttery taste. Prime rib is often roasted with a variety of seasonings and served with a side of au jus, a flavorful sauce made from the drippings of the meat. When cooked properly, prime rib is melt-in-your-mouth tender, with a rich flavor that is hard to resist.

To prepare prime rib, many cooks start by seasoning the meat with a blend of salt, pepper, and herbs, and then roasting it in the oven until it reaches the desired temperature. Some people prefer to cook their prime rib low and slow, while others prefer a high-heat method that results in a crispy, caramelized exterior. Regardless of the cooking method, prime rib is a delicious and indulgent cut of beef that is perfect for special occasions, holidays, or anytime you want to treat yourself to a delicious meal.

Ingredients

  • 4 lbs of prime rib roast
  • 1/4 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 tablespoon freshly ground black pepper
  • 1 teaspoon herbes de Provence
  • kosher salt to taste

Directions

  • Step 1

    • Place prime rib roast on a plate and bring to room temperature, 2 to 4 hours.

  • Step 2

    • Preheat the oven to 500 degrees F (260 degrees C).

  • Step 3

    • Combine butter, pepper, and herbes de Provence in a bowl; mix until well blended. Spread butter mixture evenly over entire roast. Season roast generously with kosher salt.

  • Step 4

    • Roast the 4-pound roast in the preheated oven for 20 minutes. (If your roast is larger or smaller than 4 pounds, multiply the exact weight times 5 minutes.)

  • Step 5

    • Turn the oven off and, leaving the roast in the oven with the door closed, let the roast sit in the oven for 2 hours.

  • Step 6

    • Remove roast from the oven, slice, and serve.
